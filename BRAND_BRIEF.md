# Brand & Visual Design Brief — Pistar

> **Audience:** Visual designer, brand designer, or AI design agent (Claude Code, Cursor, Figma AI).
> **Companion document to:** `BLUEPRINT.md` (technical/business spec).
> **Goal:** Define the visual identity system for Pistar — a remote ski racing video analysis platform — strong enough that anyone executing on it can produce on-brand work without further direction.
> **Languages of the product:** English (default), German, French. Italian phase 2.

---

## 1. Brand foundation

### 1.1 The product in one sentence

**Pistar is a remote video analysis platform connecting families of competitive ski racers to certified coaches across borders and languages.**

Parents film their child's run, upload it, choose a coach, and receive a professional annotated video analysis within 48–72 hours. Coaches earn supplementary income; families gain expert feedback they couldn't access otherwise.

### 1.2 Strategic positioning

Pistar sits in a deliberate gap on the market map:

- **Above** the amateur ski-tech apps (Carv, Ski Tracks) which are wearable-driven and consumer-friendly but lack expert human input.
- **Below** the all-encompassing pro-sports analytics platforms (Hudl, Catapult) which are too corporate, expensive, and intimidating for a family of a U14 racer.
- **Across** the geography of European ski racing — Pistar speaks German to Austrian families, French to Savoyards, English to Brits and Scandinavians, all in the same product.

The market position is **"premium but warm"**: serious about ski racing performance, never corporate or sterile.

### 1.3 Brand archetype

Two archetypes blended:

- **The Sage** (60 %) — the trusted expert who sees what others miss. Calm, precise, generous with knowledge. Think the experienced coach who quietly transforms a young racer's career.
- **The Hero** (40 %) — about pursuit, performance, hard work paying off. The young racer aiming for FIS points, the parent wanting their kid to flourish.

**Not the Outlaw, not the Jester.** Pistar is not a freeride/freestyle brand, not edgy, not playful in a Red Bull way. The energy is focused, mature, slightly Nordic in its restraint.

### 1.4 Brand promise

> **"Skiing better, seen clearly."**

Everything Pistar does should ladder up to this: showing the truth of a run, helping a racer improve through expert eyes, removing geography and language as barriers.

### 1.5 Personality attributes (and their explicit opposites)

| We are | We are not |
|---|---|
| **Precise** — measurements matter, details matter | Approximate, "good enough" |
| **Calm** — confident without shouting | Hyped, energy-drink-coded |
| **Premium** — careful craft, no shortcuts | Luxury / snobby / exclusionary |
| **Technical** — at ease with race jargon (radius, FIS code, side-cut) | Jargon-locked, gatekeeping |
| **International** — equally at home in Kitzbühel, Méribel and Vermont | Generic global, place-less |
| **Mature** — speaks to parents and athletes as adults | Teen-coded, gamified, emoji-heavy |
| **Optimistic** — every run is a chance to improve | Critical, harsh, judgemental |

Print these out. Pin them above the desk.

## 2. Audience emotional brief

The visual identity has to land across three very different user states. Design choices should feel right to all three simultaneously.

### 2.1 The parent (primary buyer)

A 38–52 year-old in Innsbruck, Annecy or Stowe. Engineer, doctor, executive, business owner. They drove their kid to training at 6 a.m. for the last three years. They are time-poor, worry-rich, and price-sensitive only relative to perceived quality.

**They need to feel:** "I can trust this. These are real coaches. My kid is in safe hands. The money I spend here is well spent."

**The visual should signal:** quiet expertise, careful design, no gimmicks. Like booking a good private clinic, not a Groupon spa.

### 2.2 The athlete (the user behind the product)

A 12–20 year-old competitor. Sees themselves as serious. Watches World Cup highlights on YouTube. Follows Marco Odermatt and Mikaela Shiffrin on Instagram. Cares about gear, lines, edge angles, FIS points.

**They need to feel:** "This is for real racers. This is what people who matter use."

**The visual should signal:** performance credibility, race-side authenticity, contemporary design language they recognize from premium sports tech.

### 2.3 The coach (the supply side)

A 28–55 year-old certified coach (BE, DE, FIS, ÖSV, Swiss-Ski). Often a former competitor. Used to working with traditional ski federations, club bureaucracy. Skeptical of "tech bro" platforms that talk down to coaches.

**They need to feel:** "These people respect what I do. They built this for me, not despite me."

**The visual should signal:** craft, restraint, attention to the working tools (the video player, the annotation surface). The coach dashboard should feel like Linear, not like Uber Eats.

### 2.4 Cross-language considerations

The visual identity is the **shared language** when the linguistic one switches. A German parent and a French parent should both find the product feels "made for them" without seeing translation seams.

This implies: **iconography over text where possible**, generous typography that handles long German compound words gracefully, careful spacing that doesn't break when French strings run 25 % longer than English.

## 3. Visual territory

Three creative directions to choose from (or blend). Pick one as the dominant flavor; let the other two influence accents.

### 3.1 Direction A — "Alpine Engineering"

The product as precision instrument. Think a Swiss watch crossed with a race-tuning bench. Heavy use of monospaced numerals for timing and metrics, technical line work, neutral palette punctuated by a single race accent.

**References to study:** Linear (linear.app), Stripe Press, Rapha Performance Trade-In tool, On Running's editorial pages, FIS World Cup TV graphics during downhill events.

**Tone:** restrained, exact, confident. Lots of white space. Numerals are heroes.

### 3.2 Direction B — "Northern Light"

The product as a craft. A nod to Scandinavian outdoor brands — POC, Norrøna, Houdini Sportswear. Slightly warmer, more editorial, with photography front and center. Type with character (slightly humanist), generous baselines, off-white grounds.

**References to study:** POC Sports (pocsports.com), Houdini Sportswear, Mons Royale, Norrøna, Field Magazine, Cereal Magazine layouts.

**Tone:** lyrical, atmospheric, photographic. Stories of mountains and people.

### 3.3 Direction C — "Race Day"

The product wearing its sport heritage on its sleeve. Direct visual codes from ski racing — bib numbers, gate poles, timing screens, course inspection sketches. More chromatic energy, tighter typographic rhythm.

**References to study:** Atomic Redster brand campaigns, Salomon S/Race lines, Stöckli's pro race kit, ÖSV Austria's federation graphics, Inferno Mürren and Hahnenkamm course profile illustrations.

**Tone:** energetic, kinetic, race-side. Urgent without being shouty.

### 3.4 Recommended primary direction

**Lead with Direction A (Alpine Engineering), accented by B (Northern Light), with rare touches of C (Race Day)** for product-specific moments like the booking page, the leaderboard for clubs, the race-mode comparison view.

This keeps the everyday product experience sober and trust-building, while letting domain-specific moments flex sport heritage.

## 4. Logo system

### 4.1 Wordmark — the workhorse

The wordmark is `pistar`, set in lowercase. Lowercase reads as more contemporary, more approachable, and more international than uppercase or title case across all four target languages.

**Type basis:** a custom-tuned geometric sans, derived from a base such as Söhne, Aeonik Pro, or General Sans. Custom adjustments:

- Slight contrast on the **vertical strokes of the `i` and `t`** to suggest carved tracks.
- The **`a`** uses a single-storey form (not double-storey), reading more modern and aligning visually with `r`.
- The **dot of the `i`** is replaced by a 5° clockwise-tilted square — a small visual rhyme with the slope of a slalom panel.

**Tracking:** -10 to -15 (slightly tight), but never compressed. The wordmark must breathe.

### 4.2 Symbol — the standalone mark

The symbol is an abstract glyph derived from the **letter P** combined with a single carved arc. Read as: a vertical stem (the racer) with a tight clean radius peeling off to the right (the line of the carve).

**Construction principles:**
- Built on a **5×5 grid** for mathematical cleanness.
- Stroke weight matches the optical weight of the wordmark at the same x-height.
- Single weight only at MVP; can extend to a 2-weight system later if needed (filled vs. outlined for app icon vs. favicon).

The symbol must work:
- At 16 px (favicon, browser tab).
- At 32 px (app icon corner, email signature).
- At 64 px (avatar, navigation logo).
- At 1024 px (app store, marketing hero).

### 4.3 Lockups

Three locked configurations:

1. **Horizontal lockup:** symbol left, wordmark right, with a fixed visual gap of half the symbol width. Use everywhere by default.
2. **Stacked lockup:** symbol on top, wordmark below. Use in vertical formats (mobile splash, social avatars only when required).
3. **Symbol alone:** for app icons, favicons, social profile pictures, internal merch.

**Never:** rotate the wordmark, stretch any part of the lockup, place the wordmark above the symbol, or set the wordmark in any case other than lowercase.

### 4.4 Clearspace and minimum sizes

- **Clearspace:** at least the height of the symbol on all sides.
- **Minimum wordmark width on screen:** 80 px.
- **Minimum symbol size on screen:** 16 px.
- **Minimum wordmark width in print:** 20 mm.

### 4.5 Logo don'ts

Hard rules. Pin these in the brand kit:

- Never on busy photographic backgrounds without a controlled scrim or solid backplate.
- Never rotated.
- Never stretched non-proportionally.
- Never with a drop-shadow, bevel, or gradient effect.
- Never recolored outside the approved palette (§5).
- Never in any case other than lowercase.
- Never with a tagline locked into the lockup itself — taglines live separately, set in the body face, never welded to the mark.

## 5. Color system

### 5.1 Philosophy

A **disciplined three-color core** plus a working set of neutrals. No "secondary palette" of decorative colors — that path leads to inconsistency. If something needs a color, it gets one of the core three, a neutral, or a semantic state color. That's it.

### 5.2 Primary palette

```
Glacier Ink       #0A1A2F   The dominant brand color. Deep midnight navy.
                            Used for primary surfaces in dark mode,
                            primary text in light mode, headers, primary
                            buttons. Replaces "black" everywhere.

Fresh Snow        #F8F7F4   The dominant light surface. Slightly warm,
                            off-white. Never use pure #FFFFFF — too clinical.

Race Coral        #FF5A3C   The single accent. A warm electric red-orange.
                            Used sparingly: primary CTA, important data
                            highlights, the brand's "moment of energy."
                            Never used as a large field — always as an accent.
```

Why this palette: navy + off-white + warm red is a centuries-old combo (military, sports federations, premium athletic brands) that signals trust, performance, and decisiveness without falling into Silicon-Valley-startup blue or generic ski-poster red.

### 5.3 Neutrals

```
Granite 900       #14213D   Dark text on light, alternative to Ink for body
Granite 700       #3A4A66   Secondary text on light
Granite 500       #6E7B92   Muted text, captions, metadata
Granite 300       #B5BCC9   Disabled states, dividers (medium contrast)
Granite 100       #E5E7EC   Subtle dividers, surface separations
Granite 050       #F1F1EE   Alternate light surface, slightly cooler than Snow
```

### 5.4 Semantic colors

Used only for system states. Never for branding accents.

```
Success Pine      #1F6B4A   Confirmation, "delivered", positive states
Warning Slate     #C28A2A   Pending, "awaiting upload", attention required
Error Rust        #B5351F   Errors, failed payments, blockers
Info Stone        #2E5C8A   Neutral system messages, tooltips
```

Each has a 100/300/700/900 cousin for backgrounds, borders, and text on tinted backgrounds. Designer to extend this scale; document each level in the design tokens file.

### 5.5 Dark mode

Dark mode is **first-class, not an afterthought**. Many users will analyze video at night with the lights off. Glacier Ink becomes the canvas, Fresh Snow becomes the text.

Dark mode tokens:
```
Surface 0   #050C18   Background (deeper than Ink)
Surface 1   #0A1A2F   Cards, panels (= Glacier Ink)
Surface 2   #142640   Elevated cards
Surface 3   #1F3457   Hover states on Surface 2
Text 0      #F8F7F4   Primary text (= Fresh Snow)
Text 1      #C5CAD3   Secondary text
Text 2      #8893A6   Muted text
```

Race Coral stays the same hex in dark mode but shifts perceptually warmer. Test each accent placement.

### 5.6 Accessibility

- All text/background pairs must clear **WCAG AA** at minimum, AAA for body copy where possible.
- The Race Coral on Fresh Snow background does **not** clear AA for body text. Use Coral for buttons (with white text on it) and large display elements only — never for paragraph text.
- Dark mode pairs must be tested separately. Don't trust that "if light mode passes, dark will."

## 6. Typography

### 6.1 Type system

Three faces, each with a clear role.

**Display face — Söhne** (Klim Type Foundry, paid)
For headlines, hero numbers, the wordmark base. Söhne has the technical precision of the Alpine direction with enough humanism to avoid Helvetica fatigue. Excellent multilingual coverage including German diacritics, French accents, Scandinavian characters.

> **Free alternative:** General Sans (Fontshare, free for commercial use). Slightly less refined but very capable.

**Body face — Inter** (Rasmus Andersson, free)
For all UI body copy, paragraphs, forms. Inter is the most reliable multilingual sans on the web in 2026, with exceptional German and French rendering, optical sizes, tabular numerals, and dense character coverage. Free, ubiquitous, and there is no good reason not to use it.

**Numeric / mono face — JetBrains Mono** (free)
For timing displays, FIS codes, athlete numbers, code-like data. Tabular figures, clear distinction between `0` and `O`, and `1` and `l`. Reserved for **actual data** — never used decoratively for body or display.

### 6.2 Type scale (light mode)

Mobile-first scale, expand for desktop.

```
Hero        56 / 60   Söhne Semibold     -2% tracking
Title 1     40 / 44   Söhne Semibold     -1% tracking
Title 2     32 / 36   Söhne Medium       -1% tracking
Title 3     24 / 28   Söhne Medium       0
Lede        20 / 30   Inter Regular      0
Body        16 / 26   Inter Regular      0
Body Sm     14 / 22   Inter Regular      0
Caption     12 / 18   Inter Medium      +2% tracking, uppercase
Mono        14 / 20   JetBrains Mono     0
```

Desktop heroes can scale up to 96 / 100 px on landing pages. Don't go larger — Pistar is mature, not aggressive.

### 6.3 Localization considerations

- **German** runs **15–25 % longer** than English on average and produces compound words that don't break (Geschwindigkeitsbegrenzung). Buttons must accommodate. Never set buttons with `text-overflow: ellipsis` for German strings — find a shorter translation upstream.
- **French** uses extensive accented characters. Check that `é`, `à`, `ï`, `ô`, `ç`, `œ` render at all sizes without the accents getting clipped.
- **Quotation marks** differ: English `"…"`, French `« … »` with non-breaking spaces inside, German `„…"`. Localize, don't reuse.
- **Numerals** with French use comma as decimal (35,00 €), German uses comma + space + EUR (35,00 €), English uses period and prefix ($35.00 / €35.00). Use locale-aware Intl.NumberFormat in code, never hardcoded glyphs.

## 7. Iconography

### 7.1 Style

Custom or curated set, line-based, **1.75 px stroke at 24 px reference size**, rounded line caps and joins, consistent terminal angles (90° preferred, 45° accepted).

Not chunky and friendly (not Instagram). Not hairline-thin (not Apple). Somewhere in between, technical without being austere.

### 7.2 Source

**Recommended:** Lucide (lucide.dev, free, MIT) as the foundation. Already integrated with shadcn/ui per the blueprint stack. Excellent breadth, contemporary style, easy to extend.

**Custom additions** (must be commissioned or drawn in-house):

- Ski racing gates (slalom, GS, SG, DH variants) — at least 4 custom icons.
- Discipline pictograms for filters (SL, GS, SG, DH) — bib-style or pictogram, designer's call but consistent system.
- FIS code badge.
- Annotation tool icons (line, arc, ghost, slow-motion) for the future v2 annotation editor.

### 7.3 Application

Icons sit in **24 × 24 px boxes** with a 2 px optical inset. They scale 1:1 to 16, 20, 24, 32, 40, 48 px. Don't render below 16 px.

When paired with text, icon and text label share the same line-height and a consistent gap (8 px standard).

Icons **never replace** primary CTAs — labels always appear next to icons in primary actions, even on mobile. The product is multilingual, multi-cultural, and multi-generational; icon-only buttons fail one of those audiences.

## 8. Imagery

### 8.1 Photography direction

Pistar's imagery has to feel real — not stock, not generic, not GoPro-saturated. The look is **editorial-documentary**: real coaches, real young racers, real European mountains.

**Style cues:**
- Natural light dominant. Flash only for portrait close-ups, soft and warm.
- Slightly desaturated. Not sepia, not VSCO; just one notch below "phone default."
- Cool shadows, warm highlights. The signature "alpine afternoon" feel.
- Composition leaves room for type overlay — frame subjects with negative space top-left or top-right.
- Wide and medium shots over tight close-ups. The mountain matters.

**Subjects to feature:**
- Athletes in training inspection, walking the course, side-slipping.
- Coaches with stopwatches, with iPads, talking to athletes — never just standing still.
- Equipment details (skis racked, ski tuning bench, boots being buckled, hands on poles).
- Empty courses at dawn, gates being set.
- Parents at the finish area — **only** with explicit consent and never identifiable minors without parental release.

**Subjects to avoid:**
- Hero "leg-extended" carving shots (cliché).
- Champagne celebrations at the podium (not Pistar's territory).
- Apres-ski / lifestyle shots (off-brand).
- Generic snow vistas with no human element.

### 8.2 Video treatment

Video is the **product**. Treatment matters more than for most brands.

**Hero / marketing video:**
- 24 fps, cinematic. Not 60 fps "smooth phone."
- Color graded with cooler shadows, warmer mid-tones, slight crush in blacks.
- Slow camera moves preferred over handheld.
- Score: minimal, ambient, occasional Nordic-electronic. Not orchestral, not EDM.

**In-product video (the analyses themselves):**
- Will be uploaded by users — Pistar can't control source quality.
- The player frame, controls, and annotation overlays must be designed to look intentional **on top of any video quality**, including grainy phone footage at -5°C.
- Letterbox to 16:9 inside a 1:1 or 4:3 outer frame — gives a "broadcast" feel and prevents bad aspect ratios from looking broken.

### 8.3 Illustration

**Avoid generic illustration entirely** for the MVP. No "characters", no flat-vector "people running with laptops", no isometric scenes. They will date the product within 18 months and feel inconsistent with the photographic direction.

Acceptable: **technical diagrammatic illustration** for explainer pages — annotated course profiles, line diagrams, gate spacing infographics. One illustrator, one style, used surgically.

## 9. UI design language

### 9.1 Surfaces and depth

Two-level depth maximum in light mode (background + card), three levels in dark mode (background + card + elevated card). No deeper. No drop shadows nesting drop shadows.

**Shadows:**
- Light mode: subtle `0 1px 2px rgba(20,33,61,0.06), 0 4px 12px rgba(20,33,61,0.04)`.
- Dark mode: no shadows. Use surface elevation via background-color shifts only.

### 9.2 Corner radius

**Primary radius: 8 px.** Used on cards, buttons, inputs, modals.
**Small radius: 4 px.** Used on tags, badges, small inline elements.
**Large radius: 16 px.** Used on hero containers, large media frames.
**Pill: full radius.** Used on filter chips, status pills, avatars.

No mixing. Each component picks one radius and stays consistent.

### 9.3 Spacing scale

Tailwind's default 4 px base spacing scale, with named tokens:

```
space-xs   4 px
space-sm   8 px
space-md   16 px
space-lg   24 px
space-xl   40 px
space-2xl  64 px
space-3xl  96 px
```

Vertical rhythm: maintain at least `space-md` between any two elements; `space-lg` between section blocks; `space-2xl` between page sections.

### 9.4 States

Every interactive element ships with five visual states. No exceptions.

```
default
hover       — slightly elevated or tinted, never wildly different
active      — pressed feel, subtle scale or shadow shift
focus       — visible outline (2 px Race Coral, 2 px offset)
disabled    — 40 % opacity, no pointer events
```

Focus rings are non-negotiable. Keyboard accessibility is a hard requirement. The product handles user financial transactions and minor video; failing accessibility is failing users.

### 9.5 Light mode and dark mode parity

Every component, every screen, every email, must be designed in **both modes** before sign-off. No "dark mode coming later." If a component looks good in only one mode, it isn't done.

## 10. Component-specific guidance

### 10.1 The video player (the hero)

The video player is the most-seen component in the product. It deserves disproportionate care.

- 16:9 frame, rounded 8 px corners, sits on Surface 1.
- Custom controls — never shipped browser-default. Use a player like Mux Player or Vidstack with custom theming.
- Controls auto-hide after 2 seconds of inactivity, return on mouse move or tap.
- Progress bar is a 4 px Race Coral fill on a Granite 100 (light) / Surface 3 (dark) track.
- Timecode in JetBrains Mono, tabular figures, format `MM:SS.ms` for analysis precision.
- Frame-step buttons (`<` `>`) are visible — coaches need them. This differentiates Pistar from generic players.
- Speed controls: 0.25× / 0.5× / 1× / 2×, presented as a horizontal pill switcher above the timeline.
- Picture-in-picture available for the coach view — they need to play the source while writing notes.

**Annotation overlays** (on coach delivery videos) must be readable on snow. Use Race Coral for primary annotations, Pine for ghost lines, with a soft drop-shadow matched to the foreground for legibility.

### 10.2 Coach card (in directory)

The unit users browse to choose a coach. Critical to conversion.

Anatomy from top:
1. Coach portrait (4:5 ratio, professional photograph, consistent crop).
2. Name + flag set of languages spoken (using small flag pills, 16 px).
3. Disciplines as filter pills (SL, GS, SG…) styled as small caps badges.
4. Two-line bio excerpt in Body Sm.
5. Star rating (Race Coral filled stars, Granite 300 outline) + review count.
6. Price from `35 €` in Display Title 3.
7. Primary CTA: "Book analysis" in Race Coral.

Card is 320 px wide on grid, full bleed on mobile. Hover state: subtle lift (translate -2 px Y, shadow increase). On mobile: tap to detail, no hover.

### 10.3 Booking / checkout flow

Three steps maximum, with a visible progress indicator at the top. Single column. Generous spacing. Trust cues throughout (coach name and photo always visible, "secured by Polar" badge near payment, expected delivery time prominent).

Avoid: dense form fields, multiple-column layouts, surprise required fields. Every required field should be obvious, every optional field should say so.

### 10.4 Order timeline (status page after payment)

A vertical timeline with five canonical states matching the blueprint state machine: `paid` → `awaiting upload` → `under review` → `delivered` → `closed`. Each step has a timestamp, a brief description, and a small icon. Active step is in Race Coral, completed steps in Pine, future steps in Granite 300.

This screen is where parents return repeatedly while waiting. Make it pleasant, informative, and quietly reassuring.

### 10.5 Email templates

All transactional emails (order confirmation, upload reminder, delivery notification, monthly receipt) follow the same template:

- Pistar wordmark top-left, 32 px.
- Single column, 600 px max-width.
- Hero color block (Glacier Ink in dark, Fresh Snow in light) with Title 2 message.
- Body in Inter Regular.
- Single primary CTA button in Race Coral.
- Footer: address, unsubscribe (where required), localized.

Render every email in EN, DE, FR before sending. Use React Email per the blueprint.

### 10.6 Mobile

Mobile is **at least 50 %** of traffic for parents who upload from phone. Treat mobile as the primary canvas for any user-facing flow except the coach dashboard (desktop-led).

- Touch targets minimum 44 × 44 px.
- Bottom nav for client app, top nav for marketing.
- Sticky upload progress on the upload screen — parents need reassurance during a 5-minute upload over 4G.
- Iconography always paired with labels at this scale; no icon-only on mobile.

## 11. Tone of voice (multilingual)

### 11.1 General principles

Pistar's voice is **calm, knowledgeable, kind**. Never cute. Never corporate. Never overly emotional.

Three rules:

1. **Be specific.** "Your analysis is ready" not "Great news!"
2. **Respect the reader.** They know what FIS is. Don't over-explain ski.
3. **Lead with the user benefit.** Not "Pistar offers analyses" but "See your line, drilled down by a FIS coach."

### 11.2 Voice in English

Direct, slightly understated. American or British neutral — avoid colloquialisms specific to either.

Sample microcopy:
- Button: "Book analysis"
- Confirmation: "Your video is on its way. Henrik will review within 48 hours."
- Empty state: "No analyses yet. Find a coach to get started."

### 11.3 Voice in German

Polite, precise, structured. Use Sie form by default (the parent demographic expects it). Avoid Anglicisms where a native term exists.

Sample microcopy:
- Button: "Analyse buchen"
- Confirmation: "Ihr Video wurde übermittelt. Henrik prüft es innerhalb von 48 Stunden."
- Empty state: "Noch keine Analysen vorhanden. Wählen Sie einen Coach, um zu starten."

### 11.4 Voice in French

Élégant, structuré, jamais familier. Vouvoiement par défaut. Phrases complètes, ponctuation soignée (espaces insécables avant `:`, `;`, `?`, `!`).

Sample microcopy:
- Bouton : « Réserver une analyse »
- Confirmation : « Votre vidéo a bien été transmise. Henrik la commentera sous 48 heures. »
- État vide : « Aucune analyse pour le moment. Choisissez un coach pour commencer. »

### 11.5 Forbidden phrases

Across all languages, never:

- "Awesome" / "Genial" / "Génial" used corporately
- Exclamation marks in transactional confirmations
- Emojis in product UI (allowed sparingly in marketing social only)
- "We're excited to…" / "Wir freuen uns…" / "Nous sommes ravis…"
- Anything implying the user is lucky to have us

## 12. Brand applications

### 12.1 Web — marketing

Landing pages, coach directory, marketing content. Editorial-led, photograph-forward, generous whitespace. Conversion CTAs in Race Coral, supporting CTAs in outlined Glacier Ink.

### 12.2 Web — product

Client dashboard, coach dashboard, admin. Tighter information density, system fonts where Inter doesn't render fast enough on low-end devices, careful state management. Functional first, beautiful second — but always both.

### 12.3 Email

See §10.5. Three template families: transactional (most emails), digest (weekly coach earnings, monthly client recap), marketing (sparingly, opt-in only).

### 12.4 PDF (invoices, self-billing receipts, certificates)

Generated server-side via `@react-pdf/renderer` per the blueprint. Single-page, A4. Wordmark top-left, document title top-right, body in Inter Regular, totals in JetBrains Mono. Footer with company legal info localized.

### 12.5 Social

Instagram primary, TikTok secondary, YouTube for long-form coach explainers (phase 2). Identical visual system: Race Coral on Glacier Ink for hero overlays, photographic backgrounds, JetBrains Mono for timecodes when used.

Avoid: meme formats, dance trends, brand-personality shitposting. Pistar's social is closer to **POC** or **Castelli** than to **Liquid Death** or **Ryanair**.

### 12.6 Print and physical (phase 2+)

Stickers for athletes' helmets and ski bags, club kits, race-side branding. Always in Glacier Ink and Fresh Snow only — Race Coral reserved for digital. Physical Race Coral fades and varies; physical Pistar is monochrome.

## 13. The visual no-go list

Things that are explicitly off-brand. Pin these next to the personality attributes (§1.5).

- ❌ Powder-blue + bright-yellow ski-poster aesthetic
- ❌ Mountain-icon-with-sun logo
- ❌ Stock photography of grinning teenagers in ski gear
- ❌ Slanted italic faux-speed type
- ❌ Drop shadows on text
- ❌ Gradients of any kind on the wordmark or symbol
- ❌ Generic flat illustration of "people"
- ❌ Heavy emoji use in product copy
- ❌ All-caps body text
- ❌ Hairline strokes that disappear on standard displays
- ❌ Pure white (#FFFFFF) backgrounds
- ❌ Pure black (#000000) text
- ❌ Multiple accent colors — Race Coral is the only accent
- ❌ Stretched, rotated, or recolored logo lockups
- ❌ Mascots, characters, anthropomorphic anything
- ❌ Speed lines, swooshes, or motion-blur graphics

## 15. References — what to study, what to avoid

### 15.1 Brands to study and learn from

- **Linear** (linear.app) — restraint, technical aesthetic, dark-mode mastery.
- **Stripe** (stripe.com, stripe.press) — premium without coldness, typographic excellence.
- **POC Sports** (pocsports.com) — Scandinavian discipline applied to winter sport.
- **Rapha** (rapha.cc) — editorial sport content, premium positioning, multilingual operation.
- **Castelli** (castelli-cycling.com) — Italian premium cycling, sport heritage done right.
- **On Running** (on-running.com) — Swiss precision, performance brand for adults.
- **Atomic Skis** — particularly their Redster race-program editorial material.

### 15.2 Brands to explicitly *not* imitate

- **Hudl** — too corporate, too American sports-tech, lacks warmth.
- **Strava** — too consumer, too gamified, social-network coded.
- **GoPro** — saturated, energetic-aggressive, opposite of Pistar.
- **Generic SaaS** — Notion, Asana, Monday — Pistar is not a productivity tool.
- **Clubs and federations themselves** — FIS, ÖSV, FFS sites are functionally OK but visually dated; we are not them.

### 15.3 Films / cultural touchpoints

For mood inspiration during the design phase:

- **Streif: One Hell of a Ride** (2014 documentary) — atmosphere of Hahnenkamm.
- **Cocaine Cowboys: Marco Pantani** — sport documentary tone (cycling, but transferrable).
- **Salomon TV** — the editorial film series.
- **Le Grand Bleu** colour grading — for cool blues + warm skin tones.
